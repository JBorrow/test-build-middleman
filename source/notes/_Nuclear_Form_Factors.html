Nuclear form factors

So far we have only considered point-like potentials. To obtain the matrix element for an extended charge distribution \(\rho(x)=Ze\,f(x)\) with the normalisation \(\int f(x)\,dx=1\) we can use \[\rho(x)=\int \rho(y)\delta(x-y)\,dy=Ze\int f(y)\delta(x-y)\,dy\quad\Rightarrow \quad \phi(x)=Ze\int f(y)\frac{1}{|x-y|}\,dy\] to obtain \[\begin{aligned} \mathcal{M}_{fi}&=&\left<\psi_f|\mathcal{H}_{int}|\psi_i\right> =\frac{e}{V}\int d^3x\,e^{i{\mathbf{q}}\cdot x}\phi(x)\nonumber\\ &=& \frac{e}{V}\int d^3x\,e^{i{\mathbf{q}}\cdot x}Ze\,\int f(y)\frac{1}{\left|{\mathbf{x}}-{\mathbf{y}}\right|}\,d^3y = \frac{Ze^2}{V}\int \,d^3y\,f(y)\int d^3x\,e^{i{\mathbf{q}}\cdot x}\frac{1}{\left|{\mathbf{x}}-{\mathbf{y}}\right|}\nonumber\\ &=& \frac{Ze^2}{V}\int \,d^3y\,f(y)\frac{4\pi}{|{\mathbf{q}}^2|}\,e^{i{\mathbf{q}}\cdot y} = \frac{4 \pi Z e^2}{V|{\mathbf{q}}^2|}\int \,d^3y\,f(y)\,e^{i{\mathbf{q}}\cdot y}\nonumber\\ &\equiv&\frac{4 \pi Ze^2}{V|{\mathbf{q}}^2|}F(q)\end{aligned}\] where \[F(q)=\int \,d^3y\,f(y)\,e^{i{\mathbf{q}}\cdot y}\] is called the form factor. If the distribution is point-like the form factor is \(F(q)=1\) and we recover the previous result. If the charge distribution is not point-like, then the form factor will be reduced from its value of \(1\) as the value of \(|{\mathbf{q}}|\) is such that \(1/|{\mathbf{q}}|\) is of the order of the spacial extent of the charge distribution.

A similar reasoning can be applied if we want to take the spin effect of the electron into account and we get: \[\left(\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}\right)_{\rho(x)}=\left(\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}\right)_{\rm{Mott,no\; recoil}}\left|F({\mathbf{q}})\right|^2\] This offers the possibility to investigate the charge distribution by looking at the ratio between the Mott cross section and the measured cross section. These two cross sections are plotted in fig. fig. 2.

Figure 2: The left panel shows the measured cross section and Mott cross section for the scattering of an electron of energy \(757.5\,\rm{MeV}\) off a calcium nucleus. The right-hand side panel shows the ratio of the two cross sections which is the form factor and the prediction using two different model: an homogeneous sphere of radius \(4.13\;\rm{fm}\) and a charge distribution based on eq.~eq. 6 with \(r_0=3.66\;\rm{fm}\) and \(a=0.54\;\rm{fm}\)..

By taking the ratio we get the square of the form factor \(F({\mathbf{(}}q))\). If the charge distribution \(f({\mathbf{x}})\) is spherically symmetric (\(f({\mathbf{x}})=f(|{\mathbf{x}}|)\))the form factor can be written as \[F({\mathbf{q}})=\int e^{i{\mathbf{q}}\cdot{\mathbf{x}}}f({\mathbf{x}})\,d^3x=4\pi\int f(r)\frac{\sin |{\mathbf{q}}| r}{|{\mathbf{q}}| r}r^2\,dr\] in this case the form factor only depends on the length of \({\mathbf{q}}\) so it is often written as \(F(q^2)\). Different charge distributions lead to different functional dependencies.

Figure 3: Form factor for several charge distributions

For example the form factor for an homogeneous sphere of radius \(R\) is given by \[F(q^2)=\frac{3}{a^3}\left(\sin a-a\cos a\right)\;,\quad\mbox{with}\quad a=|{\mathbf{q}}|R\] the term in parenthesis has zeros for several values of \(a\): \[\left(\sin a-a\cos a\right)=0\;\Rightarrow\;a\simeq 4.49341,7.72525,10.9041,14.0662,17.2208,\dots\] So if we assume this charge distribution and we can locate the first minimum of the form factor \(q_0\) we can infer the radius of the sphere: \[a_0=|q_0|R\simeq 4.49341\;\Rightarrow R\simeq\frac{4.49341}{q_0}\;.\] In practice it is found experimentally that the charge distribution are not homogeneous but can be described quite well using a two-parameter Fermi function as an approximation of the charge distribution:

\[ f(r)=\frac{f_0}{1+e^{\frac{r-r_0}{a}}} \qquad(6)\] where \(f_0\) is chosen such that \[4\pi\int dr \,r^2 f(r)=1\;.\] Figure fig. 4 shows the experimentally measured charge distribution1 and the approximation based on the above formula.
